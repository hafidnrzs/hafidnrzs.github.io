What is RAG?

Large language models are trained on a fixed snapshot of data. Ask them about something that happened after their training cutoff, or about your company's internal docs, and they'll either say they don't know or confidently make something up. Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) is one of the simplest fixes for this: instead of relying only on what the model memorized during training, you give it relevant information at the moment it answers.

How it works

A RAG pipeline has two main steps. First, retrieval: when a user asks a question, the system searches an external knowledge base, usually a vector database, for chunks of text that are semantically related to the query. Second, generation: those retrieved chunks are inserted into the prompt alongside the original question, and the language model generates an answer grounded in that context.

In practice, this means documents are split into chunks, converted into embeddings, and stored ahead of time. At query time, the question is embedded too, and the closest matching chunks are pulled in via similarity search before being handed to the model.

Why it matters

RAG lets you build applications that answer questions using up-to-date, private, or domain-specific information, without retraining or fine-tuning the underlying model. It also reduces hallucinations, since the model has real source material to draw from, and it makes answers more auditable, because you can trace a response back to the documents that informed it.

That combination: freshness, grounding, and traceability, is why RAG has become one of the default patterns for building practical LLM applications on top of private data.
